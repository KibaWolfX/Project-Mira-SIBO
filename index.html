📋 Morning-Of Checklist
🍽️ What to Eat at the Con

Safe bets at convention food courts:
• Plain grilled chicken or beef — ask for no sauce
• Plain rice or salad (no dressing) — add your own olive oil packet
• Hard-boiled eggs if available
• Bring your own wherever possible — it's always the safest option

Avoid at cons:
• Fried foods, sauces, marinades
• Anything with "seasoning blend" (usually contains garlic/onion powder)
• Convention energy drinks and sodas
• Shared/buffet foods where cross-contamination is high

🌅 Grab & Go Breakfasts for Busy Mornings
Some days there's no time to cook. These options are all SIBO + low-histamine safe — either fully store-bought or prepped the night before so mornings are zero-effort. Built especially for days when eating breakfast feels like too much.
🧠 Tips for Low-Energy Mornings
🌙
Sunday Prep = Whole Week of Easy Mornings
  • Bake a batch of egg muffins — freeze individually, reheat in 60 sec
  • Mix overnight oats in 4 jars — grab one each morning Mon–Thu
  • Hard-boil 6 eggs at once — keep in the fridge up to 5 days
  • Pre-portion macadamia nuts + freeze-dried fruit into zip bags
  • Make a coconut chia pudding base — add toppings day-of
⚠️
Histamine Note on Leftovers
Histamine builds up in proteins over time — even in the fridge. For low-histamine safety: cook eggs and meat fresh or freeze immediately after cooking. Don't leave cooked protein in the fridge for more than 24 hours before eating. Freeze egg muffins the same day you make them.

On busy days, breakfast doesn't have to be cooked. These are store-bought, prep-free (or prep-the-night-before) options that are fully SIBO + low histamine safe. Grab one and go.

💡 Sunday Batch Tip for Samantha
Spend 20 minutes on Sunday: boil a batch of eggs, portion out nuts + blueberries into zip bags, and prep 2–3 overnight oat jars. That covers Mon–Wed with zero morning effort.
⚠️
Watch Out For These in Packaged Breakfast
  • Honey (high FODMAP unless alfalfa/clover/raspberry type)
  • Inulin / chicory root fiber (common in protein bars — very high FODMAP)
  • Guar gum, carrageenan (SCD Illegal thickeners)
  • Onion/garlic powder in savory items
  • Sugar alcohols: xylitol, sorbitol, mannitol (big FODMAP triggers)
  • Vinegar (high histamine — avoid in morning when gut is empty)
  • Aged or processed cheese (high histamine)

Why This Matters
Autism and SIBO can be a tough combo. Sensory sensitivities around food textures, smells, and appearance make it harder to stick to a diet. These tips are designed to reduce friction and make eating feel safer and more predictable.
Food Sensory Tips
👁️
Keep Foods Separate on the Plate
Mixed foods can feel unpredictable. Use divided plates or bowls. Put sauces on the side. Knowing exactly what each bite is reduces anxiety and makes eating feel safe.
  • Bento-style boxes work great for this
  • Same color/texture foods together when possible
  • Prep meals visually the same way each time
🌡️
Temperature Consistency
Serve foods at the same temperature each time. Unexpected hot or cold can cause refusal. For SIBO, lightly warm (not hot) cooked veggies are easier to digest and more predictable texturally.
👃
Managing Strong Food Smells
Some SIBO-safe foods (fish, certain veggies) have strong smells. Tips:
  • Cook when you have time and space, not when overwhelmed
  • Use a fan or open window when cooking fish
  • Mild proteins (chicken, turkey) are often smell-neutral wins
  • Freeze-dried snacks have almost no smell
Routine Building
📅
Use the Meal Plan as a Script
The 7-day rotating meal plan gives you a predictable script to follow. Knowing what's coming reduces decision fatigue. You can even print it and check off each meal.
  • Prep Sunday: cook proteins, chop veggies for the week
  • Eat at the same times daily when possible
  • Use the shuffle button only when you feel ready for variety
🛒
Safe Food Shopping
Grocery stores can be overwhelming. Reduce friction:
  • Use the snacks list as a pre-written shopping list
  • Order from Amazon/Thrive Market to avoid stores on hard days
  • Stock backup "safe foods" you know you can always eat
  • Batch-buy non-perishables once a month
At Conventions & Social Events
🎭
Convention Food Strategy
Having a plan removes the anxiety of "what will I eat today?" Pre-packing removes almost all food decisions from an already high-stimulation day.
  • Pack the full "Con Bag" the night before, not the morning of
  • Keep snacks in an easy-to-reach outer pocket
  • Set eating alarms — forgetting to eat at cons is common
  • Bring a familiar "safe food" for comfort eating if overwhelmed
  • It's okay to step outside or find a quiet spot to eat alone
💬
Explaining Your Diet to Others
You don't owe anyone a detailed medical explanation. Some options:
  • "I have a digestive condition that limits what I can eat"
  • "I'm really particular about ingredients — I'll stick to what I brought"
  • It's always okay to say "no thank you" without explanation
Emergency Toolkit
🆘
When You're Overwhelmed and Hungry
Your 5-minute reset:
  • Step away from the crowd (outside, hallway, bathroom)
  • Drink water first — even mild dehydration worsens everything
  • Eat something from your bag — a beef stick or seaweed pack
  • Put on headphones or sunglasses if stimulation is high
  • Give yourself permission to slow down — you don't have to push through
